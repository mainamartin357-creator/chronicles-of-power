Black Gold & Blackmail: How Oil Redrew the Political Map

Published July 2026 · 20 min read · Global · 20th–21st Century

No mineral in modern history has concentrated political power as effectively as oil. It has toppled governments, funded revolutions, redrawn alliances, and turned desert kingdoms into pivotal players on the world stage.

The Birth of Petro-Politics

The political history of oil begins in earnest in the early twentieth century. The British decision to convert the Royal Navy from coal to oil locked a great power into securing Middle Eastern supplies. By mid-century, Western companies dominated production under concession systems that left host governments with a minority share of the rents.

That arrangement began to crack in the 1950s and 1960s. The decisive rupture came in October 1973.

The 1973 Embargo: Oil as a Weapon

In response to Western support for Israel during the Yom Kippur War, Arab members of OPEC imposed an embargo and coordinated production cuts that quadrupled the price of oil. The shock transferred enormous wealth to the Gulf states and demonstrated that resource nationalism could be a coherent foreign-policy tool.

Oil and the Modern Middle East

The political geography of the Middle East cannot be understood without oil. The rise of the Saudi state, the Iran-Iraq War, the two Gulf Wars, and the post-2011 regional order all have petroleum running through them as both cause and fuel.

Oil concentrates power. Because it generates large, easily taxable rents, it weakens the fiscal contract between rulers and ruled. Governments that live off oil have less need to bargain with their citizens.

The Present and the Transition

As of the mid-2020s the world is still deep in the oil age even as the energy transition accelerates. The history of oil shows how a single mineral can reorder the hierarchy of states and entangle energy with almost every major conflict of an era.
