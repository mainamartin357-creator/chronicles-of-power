The Cobalt Curse: Congo’s Endless Wars and the Battery Boom

Published July 2026 · 22 min read · Democratic Republic of Congo

More than 70 percent of the world’s cobalt — the metal that stabilizes lithium-ion batteries — is mined in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Much of it comes from the same provinces that have known almost continuous armed conflict since the 1990s. The green energy transition and the Congolese wars are the same story told from opposite ends of the supply chain.

A Geological Lottery Ticket Written in Blood

The Copperbelt that stretches across southern DRC is one of the planet’s richest mineral provinces. Cobalt occurs as a by-product of copper mining, but also in concentrations that make artisanal mining profitable. Unlike industrial copper operations, cobalt can be dug by hand, washed in rivers, and sold to middlemen.

The First and Second Congo Wars (1996–2003) were continental in scale. While many motives were at work, control of mineral-rich territories was never far from the calculations of the major players. After the formal end of the Second Congo War, the fighting simply fragmented. Today more than 100 armed groups operate in the east, many of them taxing or controlling mining sites.

“Cobalt is not just a mineral. In the Congo it is a political actor — it recruits fighters, buys weapons, corrupts officials, and keeps the international community interested enough to maintain a presence but rarely enough to resolve the underlying chaos.”

The Battery Boom Arrives

Until the mid-2010s cobalt was a relatively obscure industrial metal. The explosion of demand for electric vehicles changed everything. Prices spiked, drawing new Chinese investment and new scrutiny. Suddenly the same mines that had financed warlords for twenty years were also financing the global energy transition.

This created a profound tension. Western companies announced due-diligence initiatives while Chinese companies expanded aggressively. The result has been a geopolitical competition played out in the copper-cobalt belt.

Why the Curse Persists

  • State capacity remains extremely low in the mining provinces.
  • Neighboring states have strategic and economic interests that repeatedly draw them into Congolese territory.
  • Artisanal miners number in the hundreds of thousands. Closing unsafe sites without alternative livelihoods creates new grievances that armed groups exploit.

The cobalt curse is not a natural law. It is the product of specific historical choices and a global market willing to buy the metal without asking too many questions about the conditions of its production.
