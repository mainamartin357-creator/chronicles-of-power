Blood Diamonds: How Gems Funded Sierra Leone’s Decade of Horror

Published July 2026 · 18 min read · Sierra Leone · 1991–2002

In the alluvial gravel of eastern Sierra Leone, diamonds do not require deep shafts or industrial machinery. They lie near the surface, scattered by ancient rivers, waiting for a shovel, a sieve, and a pair of desperate hands. That geological accident made them the perfect currency of war.

The Perfect Rebel Mineral

Diamonds are small, portable, and nearly impossible to trace once they leave the ground. Unlike oil or copper, they need no pipelines or smelters. A rebel commander could wrap a handful of high-value stones in a cloth, walk them across the border into Liberia, and convert them into weapons, ammunition, and the loyalty of fighters within days.

Between 1991 and 2000, this trade sustained the Revolutionary United Front (RUF) long after most external observers expected the rebellion to collapse. The RUF, led by Foday Sankoh, invaded Sierra Leone from Liberia in March 1991 with the backing of Charles Taylor.

“The diamonds were the oxygen of the war. Without them the RUF would have suffocated within months. With them, they could breathe for a decade.”

How the Trade Worked

The chain was short and brutal. RUF units forced local populations to dig under armed guard. Production was collected at command posts, then moved by foot or vehicle to Liberia. Charles Taylor’s regime provided safe passage, airstrips, and buyers. From Monrovia the stones entered the legitimate diamond pipeline through Antwerp and other trading centers.

Estimates of the value of RUF diamond exports in the late 1990s put annual revenues at $25–125 million — enough to purchase significant quantities of arms on the regional black market.

The Human Cost

The methods used to control the mining areas became synonymous with the war’s extreme violence. Amputation of hands and limbs was a calculated tool of population control. Villages near diamond areas were emptied or turned into labor camps. By the time the war officially ended in 2002, more than 50,000 people had been killed and hundreds of thousands displaced.

International Response and the Kimberley Process

Global awareness of “blood diamonds” grew through the 1990s. Investigative journalists, NGOs, and eventually the United Nations began documenting the trade. The decisive innovation was the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme, launched in 2003. While imperfect, it marked the first systematic attempt to regulate the mineral’s political economy.

Lessons That Still Echo

  • Portability is power. Minerals that can be extracted with low technology and moved easily favor non-state armed groups.
  • Neighboring states matter. Taylor’s Liberia was the indispensable enabler.
  • The prize shapes the war. The RUF’s military strategy and atrocities cannot be fully understood without the geography of alluvial diamonds.

Today the same logic appears in other minerals: cobalt and coltan in eastern Congo, gold in the Sahel, and oil in numerous civil wars.
